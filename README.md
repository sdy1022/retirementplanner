About This App
The Retirement Strategy Calculator models retirement drawdown strategies — Roth conversions, Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs), Social Security timing, and Buy-Borrow-Die — over a full lifetime projection, then stress-tests the winning plan with Monte Carlo simulation. It replaces ad-hoc spreadsheet analysis with a repeatable model that lets you:

Snapshot your accounts — 401(k), Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and taxable brokerage.
Compare strategies side by side — a "do nothing" baseline vs. Roth conversion strategies (fixed amount, fill-to-bracket, auto-optimize, smooth income target).
Pick the right lever per bucket — the Strategy Selector decides whether Roth conversion (pre-tax dollars) and/or Buy-Borrow-Die (brokerage dollars) add value, since the two act on disjoint money.
Get a concrete action plan — the Dashboard translates the winning tax-funding strategy into year-by-year steps.
Quantify the risk — the Monte Carlo page runs the chosen plan through thousands of randomized market-return sequences (historical block bootstrap) and reports the probability the plan never runs short, plus a percentile fan chart of assets by age.

BBD vs Roth Conversion — A Strategy Selection Framework
1. Core Definitions of the Two Strategies
1. Buy, Borrow, Die (BBD)
Core logic: Never sell stocks during your lifetime (avoiding capital gains tax entirely); borrow against your securities to fund living expenses; at death, pass the "assets + loan debt" package to your heirs, using the Step-up in Basis rule to wipe out all accumulated capital gains tax.

Essence: Trade high risk (loan interest, forced liquidation in a market crash) for nominal maximization of total assets.

Who it fits: A very small number of ultra-wealthy households. Requires an extremely large brokerage account with a cost basis near zero.

2. Roth Conversion
Core logic: Proactively convert pre-tax money to Roth during your lifetime, paying the tax now at a known rate, in exchange for decades of fully tax-free growth.

Essence: Trade certainty (locking in today's known tax rate) for long-term stability, defusing future policy risk and the after-death tax bomb.

Who it fits: The vast majority of ordinary and middle-class households — especially those who want to protect a surviving spouse from the "single-filer tax penalty," or spare their children the SECURE Act burden of draining an inherited IRA within 10 years.

The first is the wealthy person's "gamble," using leverage risk to maximize assets; the second is the ordinary person's "steady play," paying a known, controllable tax today so descendants inherit fully tax-free with no strings attached.
2. The Core Decision Criterion: Which Bucket Holds Your Money?
Pre-tax (401k/IRA) >> Brokerage → choose Roth Conversion (the steady play)
Brokerage >> Pre-tax → choose Buy, Borrow, Die (the gamble)
Prerequisite: the brokerage cost basis is extremely low
Prerequisite: the brokerage is large enough that the SBLOC loan-to-value ratio stays safe
Both buckets roughly equal → lead with Roth Conversion (steadier, more controllable)
3. Two Concrete Case Studies
Case A: Mostly Pre-tax (suits Roth Conversion)
Pre-tax (401k/IRA)	$2,000,000 (67%)
Roth	$500,000
Brokerage	$500,000 (basis $250,000)
Total assets	$3,000,000
Why Roth Conversion:

$2M in pre-tax = a ticking tax bomb
RMDs at 75 force out ~$80–100k/year (assuming the balance is still $2M at 75; if today's $2M grows at 8% to age 75 with no conversions, the balance is roughly $9M and the first-year RMD exceeds $370k) → pushed into the 32%+ bracket
When one spouse dies → the survivor files single → the same RMDs at higher brackets
When the children inherit → the SECURE Act 10-year rule forces them to withdraw $200k+ per year, taxed heavily during their peak earning years
Roth Conversion solves all of the above at once
Why BBD does not fit:

The brokerage is only $0.5M — too small to run an SBLOC safely
The $2M pre-tax cannot be pledged as SBLOC collateral (prohibited by law)
Even with borrowing, the $2M pre-tax tax bomb remains — BBD cannot defuse it
Unrealized gains are only $250k — the Step-up in Basis saves little
Case B: Mostly Brokerage (suits Buy, Borrow, Die)
Pre-tax (401k/IRA)	$500,000 (17%)
Roth	$500,000
Brokerage	$2,000,000 (basis $500,000)
Total assets	$3,000,000
Why Buy, Borrow, Die:

The $2M brokerage holds $1.5M of unrealized gains
Selling outright: $1.5M × 23.8% (LTCG + NIIT) = ~$357k in capital gains tax
BBD strategy: borrow $600–800k via SBLOC (30–40% loan-to-value) to fund living expenses
The $2M stays invested and keeps compounding
At death, the Step-up in Basis erases all $1.5M+ of gains → $0 capital gains tax
Heirs inherit at market value, sell tax-free, and pay off the SBLOC
Net tax saved: $357k+
Why Roth Conversion matters less here:

Pre-tax is only $0.5M — RMDs are small and the tax is manageable
Converting $0.5M costs ~$110k in tax, saving only $50–80k versus simply paying tax on RMDs
And selling brokerage assets to pay the conversion tax triggers additional capital gains tax
4. Decision Matrix
Dimension	Roth Conversion	Buy, Borrow, Die
Where is the money?	Mostly pre-tax	Mostly brokerage
Unrealized gains	Small (irrelevant)	Huge (drives the strategy)
Cost basis	Doesn't matter	Very low = Step-up saves a fortune
SBLOC collateral	Brokerage too small = insufficient	Brokerage large = ample
RMD risk	High (large pre-tax balance)	Low (small pre-tax balance)
SECURE Act inheritance risk	High (children inherit the pre-tax bomb)	Low (children inherit stepped-up assets)
Risk tolerance required	Low (tax is known and certain)	High (leverage, market risk, margin calls)
Best suited to	Middle class and above	Ultra-high net worth
5. The Third Case: Hybrid (Both Buckets Are Large)
Scenario: $2M pre-tax + $2M brokerage (basis $0.5M)

Optimal strategy: do both

Use Roth Conversion to clean out the pre-tax bucket (defuse the RMD bomb)
Leave the brokerage untouched, reserved for BBD / Step-up in Basis
Pay conversion taxes from income or cash — never touch the brokerage (protect the BBD collateral)
Note: this requires enough cash flow to pay the conversion tax without touching either bucket. It is the ultra-wealthy version — feasible only with an independent income stream or cash reserves.

6. Bottom Line
Money mostly in the pre-tax bucket → Roth Conversion (defuse the tax bomb early)
Money mostly in the brokerage bucket with a very low basis → Buy, Borrow, Die (let the Step-up erase all gains at death)
Both buckets roughly equal → lead with Roth Conversion (steadier, more controllable, independent of markets and interest rates)
The same $3M in total assets calls for completely different strategies depending purely on which buckets hold it. The real question is never "which strategy is better" — it is "where is your money."
7. Corrections and Caveats (Important)
1. The two strategies are not either/or — they act on different buckets
IRA/401k assets get no Step-up at death (they are IRD — heirs pay full ordinary income tax), so pre-tax money can never use BBD
Brokerage money cannot be Roth-converted
The real decision is "what to do with each bucket," not "which single path the household takes"
Even in Case B, the $0.5M pre-tax should still be converted gradually in low-income years at the 10–12% brackets (small buckets convert cheaply precisely because they are small; the "$110k conversion tax" figure assumed a lump-sum conversion at 22% and overstates the cost)
2. Case B's "$357k tax savings" is measured against a straw man
It assumes everything is sold in a single year at the top 23.8% rate
The realistic alternative is "sell gradually in low-income retirement years": below roughly $97k of MFJ taxable income the LTCG rate is 0%, and NIIT only applies above $250k MAGI. Harvesting gains year by year might cost $100–200k over a lifetime, or even less
BBD's true edge over "selling smartly and slowly" is far thinner — while carrying decades of compounding loan interest, margin-call risk, and the policy risk that the Step-up rule survives unchanged for decades
3. Balances alone are not enough — the decision rule needs three more variables
The unrealized-gain fraction g (the lower the cost basis, the more the Step-up is worth)
Annual spending ÷ brokerage balance (the smaller the borrowed living expenses relative to collateral, the safer the LTV)
Years until death T (BBD is a bet that compounding interest won't outrun the tax saved — the longer T, the bigger the bet. Mathematically BBD is an old person's strategy: workable at 85, dangerous at 60)
8. Mathematical Decision Rules (One Inequality per Bucket)
Rule 1 — Pre-tax bucket: convert a dollar this year if and only if
t_now < t_exit

t_now  = the marginal rate paid converting this dollar this year (whichever bracket it fills into)
t_exit = the rate this dollar will face on the way out if never converted
       = max(marginal rate in the RMD years, the surviving spouse's single-filer rate, the heirs' rate during the 10-year window)
Judged dollar by dollar → the answer is always "convert up to the bracket where t_now catches up with t_exit," never all-or-nothing. (Paying the conversion tax from a taxable account adds a bonus: it moves tax-dragged money into a tax-free shell, worth roughly 1–3 percentage points.)

Rule 2 — Brokerage bucket: hold to death (BBD) instead of selling if and only if
c × g  >  (r − m) × T + risk premium

c = the LTCG rate actually achievable selling gradually and smartly (0/15%, not 23.8%)
g = gain fraction (1 − basis/market value)
r = SBLOC borrow rate, m = expected market return, T = years until death

Plus a hard feasibility constraint (stress test):
loan balance (with compounding) ≤ λ × (1 − d) × brokerage market value    (e.g. λ = 50% max LTV, d = 40% crash)
If breached in any year, check whether backstop assets can cure it; if not, BBD is infeasible no matter how good the math looks
Two key points about collateral: (1) Only the brokerage can appear in the LTV denominator — IRAs/Roths cannot legally be pledged as loan collateral (IRC §408(e): pledging an IRA is treated as a distribution, blowing up the account); (2) but Roth/cash can serve as a backstop: if a stress breach occurs and the backstop is large enough to bring the loan back under the LTV cap, a margin call downgrades from "forced liquidation at the bottom" to "an expensive cure paid with tax-free assets" — BBD remains feasible, at the cost of burning your most precious tax-free space to sustain leverage.

Household-level rough comparison (where the "look at the buckets" heuristic comes from)
Conversion value ≈ P × (t_exit − t_now)          P = pre-tax balance
BBD value        ≈ B × g × c − rate-spread cost   B = brokerage balance
Balance is only one factor in the product: a $2M brokerage with a high cost basis (g ≈ 0.1) still shouldn't do BBD; a $2M pre-tax balance owned by someone permanently in the 37% bracket (Δt ≈ 0) still shouldn't convert.

Decision Output
Condition	Verdict
Conversion value > 0 and BBD value > 0 and LTV feasible	Do both
Only conversion value > 0	Roth Conversion
Only BBD value > 0 and feasible	Buy, Borrow, Die
Neither holds	Do neither (spend directly, sell directly)
